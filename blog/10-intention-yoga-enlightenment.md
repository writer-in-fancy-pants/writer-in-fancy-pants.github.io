## Background
It was hard to put a cohesive question that's'answered. "How to live?", "What have I learned about life?", "Are there any principles that hold paramount importance in my opinion?", "Is there a systematic way someone else can experience the same things that I do?", etc. Eventually, I settled on "What is a practice?".

## Practice
Practice holds spiritual, physical, and mental connotations - I repeat certain actions that I have discovered over time or serendipitously found and adopted. I practice the most basic dance moves, visualizing moving with a partner. Most mornings, I show up for myself, repeat a sequence of poses and counterposes in Ashtanga Yoga tradition. I make tools that read my mental state through EEG signals and tell me how my meditation sessions went, and other tools that train a specific target like Heart Rate Variability (HRV) or Theta / Alpha via live feedback that have been shown to have outsized impact on wellbeing, creativity, Autonomic Nervous System (ANS) activity, flow state, peace, etc in various peer reviewed studies.

Once conceptualized, a practice needs to ripen to bear fruit. The quality of practice depends on the same factors as a flow state - it should bring you to the edge of your current ability, and operate there. That is where you are actually engaged, not bored and not tired, and holding attention on the practice is easier, more natural. A well designed practice adjusts itself to your mental and physical state. In yoga terms - Tapas. Like applying heat to a metal to purify it, you put in effort - enough to soften and re-align habitual patterns, but not enough to burnout or create unanticipated patterns. Patience and consistency is the key.

## Attention / awareness
Practices can be layered. In particular, mindfulness is a practice in awareness that makes every other practice meaningful and aligned. Various mindfulness exercises have had wondrous effects on the way I approach the world. Compared to a few years ago, I am more aware of my motivations and feelings at any given instant, an outcome I attribute to the mostly inconsistent awareness practice. FYI, anyone claiming to always be fully aware and still addicted to smoking cigarettes, is very likely lying, more to themselves than to you. 

More and more, I treat attention and awareness as the same. They end up being circular every time I try to define them. Attention - the capacity to be aware of X. Awareness - paying attention to X. Attention is the currency of awareness. Awareness is consciousness itself. If you are not aware at a moment, sleeping for example, you can extrapolate and intellectualize that you were alive, but there is no you present to experience that. Only the moment I'm aware am I really living. It is an active process. 

Attention is limited, but it can trained, enhanced. As you practice awareness, of the body for example, it becomes easier. Comes more naturally. That is what a practice is. Training awareness. So much so that eventually it becomes who you are, that the extraneous 'What if's, 'How's, and the numerous conditionals do not need mental processing. They become intuitive. That is a ripened practice.

## Gratitude
Practicing awareness is literally remodeling an airplane while it flies. It is a marvel that human beings have this capability by default. More often than not, I take it for granted. Same as the air I breathe. And the produce and groceries and products I rely on. Rarely, occasionally, I do appreciate the incredibly connected nature of the world, the scale of the systems underpinning the simplest necessities and pleasures of life.

I keep coming across this idea of gratitude. Of being grateful for the things and people you do have. It truly is a powerful concept - bringing awareness away from what you don't have, to what you do. It realigns priorities. If you only focus on what you don't have, you manifest a hole in your life, one that never fills. On the other end, if you are only focused on what you do have, you will not want for anything. Seriously. 

If you imagine a scenario where you are hungry, but don't have food, then you are already away from the extreme of gratitude. Being thankful for what you have, means you thank the food that comes to you, and consume it. It doesn't mean inaction. Specially if one reaches that extreme through practice, they would also have practice in obtaining the materials, cooking them, consuming them. One needs no thought to do the activities ingrained in oneself, like any other animal. But they'll want not, were the sustenance to be taken away. Again, like a deep meditative or flow state.

A more even-tempered version of gratitude, one practicable in social life, requires the same balance as the Autonomic Nervous System. As breathing, each inbreath excites, adding energy, readying the body for action, for achieving dreams and hopes and ambitions. Equally, each outbreath relaxes, bringing one back in touch with the ground, with what already is, with all the blessings and love and good in life. Do not be a cortisol fueled SNS zombie.

## Health / wellbeing
Health is paramount. Wellbeing - state of being well. Being, existing, living, present awareness in a well functional state. 

### Bodymind
First of all, body and mind are not separate. It makes no sense to separate them given their massive interconnectivity. Theoretically, physical deals with substance reality, mental deals with thoughts and ideas. But physical infirmity and discomfort affects the mind. Mental peace or turbulence degrade physical action. Psychological issues manifesting as aches, shortness of breath, digestive disorders is normal. Fever, poor posture, lack of physical space or comfort or safety, all directly impact the mental landscape.

Everything I've learnt of trauma (The body keeps the score), human development (), addiction (In the realm of hungry ghosts) point to a unified bodymind. Children that do not get safe environment and emotional connections find their mental capacities limited / misaligned - attention disorders, incomplete brain development, weaker impulse control, emotional regulation, and more. 

Older cultures, traditions with deeper exploration inwards, all point to the interconnectedness. African philosophy in dancing goes from learning movements (physical), performing a choreography (mental facing outwards), expressing yourself through the dance (mental facing inwards), and being in flow (spiritual). Asian cultures have numerous exercises precipitated through millenia to center mind and body, to understand oneself, and rid one of suffering.

### Personal Experience
I used to practice Tai Chi, learned Swing and Salsa and Zouk, and recently went deep in Yoga. Be it dance or other movement routines, body awareness training involved is an essential component for being healthy. I have found Vipassana, Anapana, and Mindful Zen meditation practices create this bodymind connection as well - with breath as the medium. There are likely numerous other practices - chanting, prayers, certain sports, partnerwork, sex, psychedelics, pilates. Atleast one of them needs to be practiced - repeated, made part of life, actively engaged in, with intention to be more aware of the body that one calls oneself. Social dance has the marvelous side effect of fulfilling the social needs in an increasingly isolating human experience. Yoga has the years of refinement and depth of information to engage deeply with each and every part of yourself. You only need one.

Being dissociated, feeling like an alien in one's own body, muted experience, selective blankness, being stuck reliving the past, are all horrible fates that those who experience them try to run away from. Those who do not experience such horrors never realize the toll it takes, the sheer amount of effort required for a relatively normal existence, and how to cope with such a thing if it happens to them. I can only implore you to educate yourself and take better care of the body and mind.

## Building a Practice
A practice - both a single instance/session, and the whole sequence of instances - have key components
* Motivation - What brings us to the practice?
* Intention - Expectations from the practice. What will guide our attention while engaged?
* Action - What is the body doing? What is the mind doing? Where is the awareness?
* Accumulation - Outcomes, notable results from the practice. Notes for other practice / instance.

An intermediate yoga practice has 3 principles - Tapas, Swadhyaya, Ishvar Pranidhan. I will be using these.
* Tapas - the tempering, the intentional difficulty, the aware active involvement in the action. 
* Swadhayaya - study of the self / understanding oneself. 
* Ishvar Pranidhan - God's will. Letting go of that which isn't in your control. The humility to identify the limits of oneself, and the vastness of the universe. Surrendering to the world and gaining freedom in that act.

### Motivation (Swadhyaya)
Overcoming the inertia. Often the most difficult step. Sometimes the first motivation comes easy - a new year resolution, a heartbreak, a friendly invitation, curiosity, interest, extra energy, chance encounter... A continued practice retains some component of this initial motivation, that drives the practice.

I find myself demotivated to practice sometimes. In those instances, sometimes showing up anyway and starting the practice dissolved the inertia. Just "Showing up" can be a powerful substitute motivation in absence of one. Motivation is very much a part of who we are. Sometimes I realize my motivation or lack thereof clearly indicates my mental/physical state that I am quite prone to be oblivious to.

Being Healthy, or Building Discipline, or It is fun, are all motivations.

### Intention (Swadhyaya, Tapas)
The preparation component. Imagine intention as all the work one does before an important business meeting. Except, the important business is our life. The outcome of a practice is often in line with the intention. The ideas explored, the direction and mindset to approach a practice, and the principles that guide awareness as it inevitably flags in a session, are all part of intention.

Keep the intention simple - short words, or an image, or whatever works. Work to explore the depth and breadth but limit the scope of each session to identifiable short phrases. Our attention is limited. We can barely pay attention to 3 active instruments in a piece of music. Ensure that the intention fits the attention. This is my rule of thumb. All the work before the session is exactly to create a relevant intention.

Effort on intention should scale with the importance of a particular practice session to you. If it is a one off, something involving a limited resource, a bit more work on intention has often provided meaningful gains, and made me thank myself for having done so. 

In my yoga practice, I notice the pain or muscular exertion, overall health, and my current ability. Intention for next session could focus on adding new poses, or consistency, or awareness, or improving a particular movement, or deepening the breath, or releasing tension.

### Action (Tapas, Ishvar Pranidhan)
Be present. The action is meaningful in and of itself - literally the end goal of the practice. If I were enlightened, this is the state I imagine to always be in. Active, engaged with the world, with reality, and doing the action as per nature without intruding thoughts, insecurities, or even effort on my part.

Gita says - "Karmanye vaadhikaaraste, maa faleshu kadaachan." Focus on the action, let go of the results. A distillation of Tapas and Ishvar Pranidhan - aware action and letting go of the hypothetical image from a psychological future. This one principle increasingly resonates with me. There are versions of it in all cultures. Bible says "True faith acts (James 2:17) and works, with labor done for the Lord—out of love and service—never being in vain. Rely on His strength, not your own, for rest and effectiveness." More religious sentiment on essentially the same concept.

True action is not based on memory, knowledge, or purpose. It is immediate (not delayed by thought) - J Krishnamurti. Thinking about an action is an activity. I think and plan in the intention stage. When acting, there's no place for thought. Intention redirects the attention. Of course, thoughts come unbidden, often. That too is a practice in itself, to rid oneself of overthinking, to train the thoughts and mind. Deeper the practice, less you need to think of the actions, and more you need to bring back the distracted mind back to the actions.

### Accumulation (Ishwar Pranidhan, Swadhyaya)
Processing the outcomes, observed highlights if any. Depending on the practice, there may be additional intellectual activity to rewire the motivations and drive the next set of intentions. Feedback, self or guided, can do wonders for advancing one's practice. Particularly in group tasks, like couple dancing, or team sports, accumulation improves chemistry and coordination.

## End goal
To be free. A debatable goal, for it assumes I am bound. Experiences over time have led me to agree to the assertion that once you attain clarity, you know the right from wrong. When thoughts do not bind you, when your understanding and practice ripens, when you are one with the nature of being, action arises spontaneously, and the result will only lead to a virtuous cycle of task alignment. I also believe one cannot be liberated of thought through thinking. One can get close, but the eventual jump happens when and if. One can only leave the window open, the wind blows on its own. This too, is a combination of Tapas, Swadhyaya, and Ishwar Pranidhan. Change what you can, accept what you cannot, and know the one from the other - summarizing the Prayer of Serendipity. Other goals work too. Goals are thoughts. Practice is a union.
