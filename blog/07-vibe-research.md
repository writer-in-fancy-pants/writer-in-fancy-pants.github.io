I came up with the term **Vibe Research** to describe to a friend how I had been spending my free time to learn about the most random things. Like Vibe Coding, there is a back-and-forth with one of the LLM tools, and the end result is often something useful and intended even if you didn't know all the steps at the onset.

## Discovery process
My process involves a topic I am at the least vaguely familiar with. More often, it is yet another theme from multiple conversations, something often experienced and discussed but not completely understood or intellectualized. There is depth to be explored that would require access to experts, hours spent familiarizing with and categorizing resources, and then pruning the available choices before the process is repeated. 

Learning a trade has always worked this way, so has research. Given the time consuming nature of the process, there is an explicit/implicit filtering mechanism right at the outset - passion/interest, cost/benefit analysis, time constraints, location, access to resources, and numerous other compromises. At every iteration, these filters restrict or outright stop the process of discovery.

## Types of Questions
Imagine the space of all questions. These filters transform and reorient the space by their very definition, and then the constraints define the boundaries around all the problems that do get solved, all the questions that are answered - Nicola Tesla with passion and luck could invent AC current; most of us answer "What's for breakfast?" every day one way or the other; Darwin took a boat as a naturalist at 22, his experiences on the journey shaped the very questions he had; and 'will food/stock/commodity/fuel price go up or down' decide the mortal fate in our capital oriented world.

In this space, there are quite a few gaps, the questions going unanswered. Shrink it down - only the questions that were asked, pondered over, formulated, and then sacrificed at the filter. Idle musings, daydreams, messy complicated multi-dimensional problems of little import, sparks that titillate mind with an absolute joy for a moment before fading, and the bread and butter hypotheticals of the poets, the writers, the explorers, the scientists, the jacks-of-all-trades - the children who don't know any better than to ask yet lack that framework, the patience, and the will to see the question through before another whim takes them over. These are the questions ripe for Vibe Research.

After all that rhetoric, I would like to point out that it is also bullcrap. Vibe research is possible because the filters have changed, that we have tools that allow use to venture further into the question space, and allow us flights of fancy that are grounded in the access to query universal human knowledge. That prose above is supposed to communicate the kind of topics I focus on, that are now open and ripe for discovery should you embrace the natural language tools that can collect, parse, correlate, analyze, and coherently present the answers. Of course, they hallucinate, but so did the village elder visualizing diseases as bad spirits, communing with the land, and answering with a herbal concoction with certifiably benevolent spirits in them. Or you know, a doctor with an abysmal lifestyle dreaming of fixing everything with pills.

## My Process
The questions come to you. Chaos is part of the process. I hold myriad interests, flights of fancy take me, and I end up on a tangent quite often. I like to notice things, be aware of the possibilities, and nowadays try to engage with the world more with my body. When you are open, letting life happen, these questions come to you. Again, none of this may be necessary. I posit that curiosity is an essential human trait.

Once I have a question, or an observation, I write it down. Sort of conditioning myself towards learning more. I read. I listen. I observe. Four out of five mental activities as per yoga - Pramana, Viparyaya, Vikalpa, Smriti - contribute to the intellectual framework of the world. And then there is experience. A mathematician may intellectually know the 5th dimension and beyond, but that is not experiential. A sportsperson may not be able to verbalize the laws of physics or biological processes, yet the experiential knowledge enables them to catch the ball or hit it far. 

You need all of these - curiosity, experience, intellectual understanding, and of course access to a fairy godmother that speaks your language and uses lightning magic on chiseled stone to divine truths any related stones have ever seen.

## My Actual Process
I'll try to be as detailed as possible in what I really employ on a daily basis.

### A free-tier chat interface. 
I prefer Claude (Sonnet 6), though Deepseek, OpenAI, Gemini, and other models are fine as well. You do need something with web search and tool calling. Alternatively, you can target a specific knowledge store like Wikipedia, or even setup a curated local knowledge repository. I am in process of doing this as well - a slow process since curation requires me to actually read and choose the content, though I sometimes cheat and often rely on expert opinion on really thick academic books. Regardless, search-engine accessible internet is what I work with on the daily basis for Vibe Research.

### Almost well formulated hypotheses
Try to be concise. There are prompt engineering tools out there, but hey, my process works well for me. It is important to understand your own question pretty well before asking. That is where the learning happens in my opinion. Using the accurate terminology, task-specific phrases, and the relevant business dialect indicates your current understanding of the topic. You can cheat on it and get your prompt polished, but I do not. I rely on the training of the models to bridge the understanding gap and meet me at my understanding level. It works well with the newer models, but prompt rewriting is certainly an option with smaller models, specially if you are not bound by free tier limits. 

Next, use short sentences, each describing an independent related task. Efficiency is key here - do not waste tokens. I try to be direct, concise in each task, and club as many related tasks together as I can while keeping them coherent and connected to each other. I also present the tasks in sequential order in the phrasing. This goes back to the previous point - the prompt should reflect the existing understanding of the topic. Except leaving out some ideas and understanding that you will use to verify and identify hallucinations. Like a train-test split while training a Machine Learning model. But I, as the operator, needs to know what I know, and how I present that.

Formulating the hypotheses has the additional advantage of minimizing compute cost. Just a bit more thinking, and the tasks are clearer, both in my head and hopefully to the LLM agent. It also helps me learn, which is the key goal here. More I think, more connections I make, and the better I digest the output. If the LLM is thinking for me, which happens sometimes, I find myself not really relating to the answers, nor retaining much of the output. Just my two cents.

### Output preference
If you do not specify how you'd like the results organized, the results can surprise you. Or be a mess to deal with. In which case, you'd end up making another request. No big deal. Anyway, I find that js artefacts and markdown results are usually good for research reports. Getting comparison tables makes the models dial down on verbosity. I am still looking into this myself, open to suggestions.

For code output, I often request the changes be presented as a diff, which reduces the output tokens drastically. Also forces me to understand the code if I want to continue updating it. Again, takes the burden of iteration from the model, and makes me work more. And more learning for me.

### Resource Management
Of course, we come around to this. Free tier means I run out of queries all the time. This is honestly a feature. The information produced with a few well-written questions is quite a lot to digest. The forced pauses mean I actually read through all the answers, update my mental model, formulate new questions for when the resource is available again. Remember, Vibe Research is not the main task in life. There are other things to do. So, they get done. Also teaches me patience and letting go, but it might just be my "grapes are sour" thinking. 

### Understanding / Learning / Knowledge
This is all me. How I behave, how I empower myself, engage with the world, be curious, be open, be loving. Yes, caring enough to spend time in doing the process above, doing it right. I firmly believe that all things worth doing take effort. It feels like human wiring. More effort you put, sweeter the reward. This has been bastardized by the current world order with the marginal costs and passion pay and asymmetric power parity and what not. As a wise man told me - it is the mentality, bro.

Everything worth caring about is also messy, complex, nuanced, and often emotionally charged. Climate change is up there on the list. So is market dynamics, human psyche, lifestyle choices, systemic inequalities, trauma, spirituality, stress, family, helping the world, and living a fruitful meaningful life. I am still learning. Learning to learn, and also learning to let go and just be.

For each result, I read through and add my own comments to it, usually in bold. Or highlight the key points.

## Afterword
It is worth doing. It is crazy really, the whole process - waking up every day, with new questions, and having it answered systematically from a universal corpus, in minutes - is peak sci-fi. It enriches my life. And those around me. It's like a wishing well. Only, like every creative writing class tells us, you need limitations and explore a concept to its fullest for an interesting story. I find it easy to get lost in the endless pursuit of novelty. LLM token spending may be infinite, but my attention is limited so that is what I work with. Constructive feedback is always welcome.
