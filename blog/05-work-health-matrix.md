This synthesis draws on peer-reviewed studies, CDC data, and large-scale cohort research to map how your job, income, and daily habits shape your disease risk — and what that implies for actionable decisions.

tl;dr - Sleep and circadian rhythm are supremely important. Drink water regularly, before you feel thirsty. A semi-regular physical activity goes a long way, but so does the choice in doing it, it shouldn't be forced/inflexible/circumstantial. Consider continued wellbeing when choosing a career/job, mental aspect too - 25-50% psychiatrists are burned out. Lack of control over one's life links to psychosomatic issues.

## I. Cancer Risk by Occupation

An estimated 2–8% of all cancer diagnoses globally are caused by exposures to carcinogens in the workplace. While safety regulations in a few countries have reduced some exposure, substantial variation persists across professions.

For example, a study of nearly half a million cancer deaths between 2020 and 2023 among US adults aged 20–64 found the highest mortality rates for males in construction and extraction occupations, and for females in transportation.

**High-risk occupational categories:**

The IARC classifies shift work involving circadian disruption as a probable carcinogen. Studies among nurses and flight attendants reveal heightened breast cancer incidence in those who worked night shifts for extended periods — mechanistic links include suppressed melatonin, altered estrogen metabolism, and impaired DNA repair.

Repeated exposure to diesel exhaust raises the risk of lung and bladder cancer among truck drivers and railroad workers. For airline crew, cancer risk stems from frequent high-altitude flights, which increase exposure to cosmic ionizing radiation, and studies have linked this to higher rates of melanoma and breast cancer.

Of cancers attributable to occupational exposures in Britain, the five leading malignancies were lung cancer (40%), non-melanoma skin cancer (21%), breast cancer (14%), mesothelioma (14%), and bladder cancer (4%). Forty-one percent of occupation-attributable cancers came from the construction industry.

Women in certain jobs — particularly hairdressers, beauticians, and accountants — may be at higher risk for ovarian cancer, while nurses may be at decreased risk. Working for more than 10 years as a hairdresser tripled ovarian cancer risk; working as an accountant doubled it.

Among women, those in occupations requiring high levels of education tend to have a higher age at first childbirth and elevated breast cancer incidence. **Night-shift work was classified as probably carcinogenic, with human evidence based on breast cancer research.**

**Sedentary office work** carries its own risk: studies have found that those who sat for more than 8 hours a day with no physical activity had a risk of dying similar to the risk posed by obesity and smoking.

## II. Psychiatric Disorders and Burnout by Profession

**Prevalence across industries:**

The American Psychological Association found in 2023 that workplace stress "remains at a concerning level, with 77% of workers having reported experiencing work-related stress in the last month." Additionally, 45% of workers feel "emotionally drained" from their work and 51% feel "used up" at the end of the workday.

Overall, 14.2% of participants in a large US study reported lifetime depression and 9.6% reported frequent mental distress. **Workers in the arts, design, entertainment, sports, and media had 1.32 times higher prevalence of frequent mental distress than the reference group.**

The healthcare and social assistance sector had the highest prevalence of having at least one day of poor mental health in the past 30 days (43.3%), while the rate of occupational anxiety, stress, and neurotic disorders was highest in the transportation, warehousing, and utilities sector.

**Suicide rates by occupation** (CDC data cited by APA): The 20 occupations with the highest suicide rates, from highest to lowest, were: farmworkers, fishermen, forestry (85 per 100,000); construction and mining trades (53); installation, maintenance, repair (48); factory production workers (35); architects and engineers (32); police, firefighters, and protective services (31); arts, design, entertainment, and media (24); computer and mathematics (23).

The CDC reports that the extraction industry (oil, gas, minerals) has among the highest suicide rates: two times higher than the national rate for males and three times higher for females, and the construction and extraction group had the highest drug overdose death rates (162.6 deaths per 100,000 workers).

**Healthcare worker burnout:**

A systematic review and meta-analysis found that a quarter to half of psychiatrists revealed burnout, with participant age, gender, and survey year not affecting burnout rates.

The APA's 2023 Practitioner Pulse Survey found that more than a third (36%) of psychologists feel burned out — down from a peak of 47% in 2021, but still indicating a significant proportion experiencing unhealthy levels of stress. More than half reported having no openings for new patients.

**Mental health and income:**

Depression prevalence decreases with increasing family income overall and in both females and males. Depression prevalence was higher in females (16.0%) than males (10.1%) overall and in nearly every age group.

The unemployed group reported more use of psychotropics and higher rates of anxiety and depression than the employed group, with lower functioning and health overall.

## III. Psychosomatic Diseases

Psychosomatic illness sits at the intersection of occupation-driven stress, mental health, and physical disease.

**Stress, anxiety, depression, and childhood trauma contribute to IBS symptomatology. Women with IBS report higher levels of psychological distress compared to men, and the gut–brain axis plays a central role in modulating both bowel function and pain perception.**

Anxiety and depression are closely related to the initiation, development, and progression of IBS, and the two mental disorders may exacerbate both gastrointestinal and extra-gastrointestinal symptoms by altering visceral sensation and influencing the microbiome–gut–brain axis.

High-demand, low-control jobs — common in healthcare, social services, and manual labor — are particularly associated with somatization. **The bidirectional nature of this relationship means that treating IBS without addressing psychological triggers often fails.**

## IV. Physical Activity and Daily Motion by Profession

White-collar workers are at greater risk of low occupational physical activity and high sedentary time. The majority of correlates found to be associated with occupational physical activity and sedentary time were intrapersonal and non-modifiable.

High amounts of sedentary behavior are directly related to many negative health outcomes, including obesity, certain cancers, type 2 diabetes, cardiovascular disease, and poor mental health. **Paradoxically, unlike leisure-time physical activity, high levels of *occupational* physical activity are related to increased risk for long-term sickness absenteeism, cardiovascular mortality, and all-cause mortality.**

This "physical activity paradox" is critical: manual laborers are physically active but often experience *worse* long-term health outcomes than moderate exercisers who choose their activity. Higher amounts of moderate- to vigorous-intensity *leisure* physical activity can attenuate the detrimental association between sedentary behaviour and health outcomes.

A 2022 employee health report from a Fortune 500 IT company revealed that the prevalence of metabolic syndrome among long-term sedentary programmers was as high as 32%, significantly exceeding the 15% rate in the general population.

## V. Sleep Duration and Quality by Profession and Income

The CDC examined data across 93 occupation groups and found that the prevalence of short sleep duration ranged from 21.4% among air transportation workers to 58.2% among communications equipment workers.

Short sleep duration (less than 7 hours per day) has been linked to adverse health outcomes including cardiovascular disease, obesity, diabetes, depression, and anxiety, as well as safety issues related to drowsy driving and injuries.

**Shift work** is among the most damaging occupational sleep disruptors. Among permanent night workers, sleep efficiency, difficulties awakening, and non-refreshing sleep all increase after night shifts, and more consecutive night shifts are associated with shorter total sleep time.

**Income and sleep have a non-linear relationship:** Full-time prime-age male workers in the top income quartile sleep about 30 minutes less than those in the lowest quartile, with this gap largely offset by time spent on other leisure activities. Paradoxically, lower income levels may offer a protective effect on health through longer sleep duration.

Work intensity — including multitasking and time pressure — has a strong positive association with poor sleep quality, an association even stronger than that between long work hours and sleep quality.

## VI. Sugar Intake and Diet Quality

Among adults aged 20 and older, foods consumed by adults who attended or graduated college contained 7.24 teaspoons of added sugar per 1,000 calories, compared with 8.37 for those without a high school degree and 8.52 for high school graduates who did not attend college. Despite a decline in density over time, average intake across all groups remained above recommendations.

A meta-analysis revealed that those with higher socioeconomic status were less likely to have high sugar-sweetened beverage intake, tending to have better access to various resources and food choices compared to those with lower SES.

At all income levels, diet quality is much lower than what might indicate a healthy diet. On a given day, only 2% of Americans meet the recommendation for whole grains, 12.5% for fruits, and 13% for vegetables — suggesting that most Americans, regardless of income, eat poorly.

Young adults with lower subjective social status in their environment consumed fewer daily servings of healthy foods and more daily servings of high-fat/high-sugar foods.

## VII. Hydration

Poor hydration was associated with biological acceleration of age, higher risk of chronic disease, and earlier mortality. When water intake shortfalls occur, dehydration takes place, and this negatively affects cognitive performance.

**Reduced physiological hydration status was associated with greater reductions in global cognitive function over a 2-year period in older adults with metabolic syndrome and overweight or obesity.**

Physiological hydration status (measured via serum osmolality), rather than simple water intake behaviors, was associated with cognitive performance and decline over time — meaning it's not just how much you drink, but how well hydrated you actually are that matters for brain function.

## VIII. Key Inferences and Lifestyle Decisions

Drawing these threads together, research supports the following evidence-based decisions:

**1. Protect circadian rhythm above almost everything else.** Night-shift work is simultaneously a psychiatric risk factor (depression, anxiety), a cancer risk (IARC Group 2A carcinogen), and a metabolic disruptor. If you do shift work, limiting consecutive night shifts, using light therapy, and anchoring sleep times reduce but do not eliminate risk.

**2. Distinguish occupational activity from leisure activity.** Manual laborers who are physically exhausted at work but sedentary after hours have *worse* cardiovascular outcomes than desk workers who exercise in leisure time. The evidence is clear: 150–300 minutes of moderate-intensity *chosen* exercise per week, structured as leisure, is what protects health. Occupational exertion without recovery is harmful.

**3. Treat high-stress work as a chronic disease risk.** Sustained occupational stress drives IBS, cardiovascular disease, immune suppression, and cancer progression. High-demand, low-control jobs are particularly toxic. Concrete countermeasures with evidence include: autonomy-seeking (asking for more control over task sequencing), peer social support at work, and boundaries on after-hours digital communication.

**4. Income does not automatically buy better health behaviors.** Higher earners do eat somewhat less sugar and have better diet quality on average, but they also sleep *less* (trading it for leisure and social activities) and often have highly sedentary jobs. The protective effect of income is real but partial and not automatic.

**5. Sleep is a professional skill, not a luxury.** Longitudinal data show that 30 additional minutes of sleep per night increases labor force participation and self-reported efficiency. Professions with the worst outcomes — construction, transportation, healthcare — share sleep deprivation as a common thread. Work intensity (time pressure, multitasking) damages sleep quality even when hours are adequate.

**6. Dehydration is a cognitive hazard at work.** Physiological dehydration (not just subjective thirst) is associated with sustained attention deficits and longer-term cognitive decline. Office workers in climate-controlled environments are chronically mildly dehydrated. Drinking before thirst — particularly if your work involves decision-making — is supported by the evidence.

**7. Sugar reduction shows a gradient with education, not just income.** Since education is the stronger predictor of lower sugar intake, efforts to improve food literacy (reading labels, understanding glycemic load) are more effective than simply having more money to spend. The "food desert" explanation for poor diets is real but only partial.

**8. Mental health screening should be profession-specific.** Artists, emergency workers, extractive industry workers, healthcare workers, and transportation workers face structurally elevated psychiatric risk. GPs and employers in these industries should proactively screen rather than wait for self-referral, which is systematically lower in high-stigma occupations.
